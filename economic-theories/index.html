Category Key Figures Layman Explanation Technical Insight
Classical Economy Adam Smith (1723–1790), David Ricardo (1772–1823) If everyone makes what they’re best at and trades, all get richer (e.g., one farmer, one shoemaker). Markets self-regulate; specialization boosts efficiency; comparative advantage benefits trade.
Marxian Economy Karl Marx (1818–1883) Workers and owners are always in conflict; system will change to share wealth equally. Capitalism breeds class conflict, leading to a classless society.
Game Theory John Nash (1928–2015) Your best choice depends on what others do (e.g., two shops choosing prices). Strategic decisions depend on others’ choices; Nash equilibrium formalizes balance.
Neoclassical Economy Alfred Marshall (1842–1924), George Stigler (1911–1991) Prices rise if demand is high and supply is low, and fall if the reverse (e.g., mango season). Supply, demand, and marginal analysis set prices; markets generally efficient.
Keynesian Economy John Maynard Keynes (1883–1946) In bad times, government should spend more to keep people working (like giving construction jobs). Government spending stabilizes recessions; demand drives short-term output.
Supply-Side Economy Arthur Laffer (1940– ) Cutting taxes can make people work and invest more, growing the economy. Lower taxes and deregulation spur growth; Laffer curve shows optimal tax rate.
Monetarism Milton Friedman (1912–2006) Too much money in the system causes prices to rise; control money to control inflation. Money supply control is key to inflation and stability; minimal intervention preferred.
Development Economics Abhijit Banerjee (1961– ), Muhammad Yunus (1940– ) Small loans or targeted help can lift poor people out of poverty (e.g., starting a shop). Poverty alleviation via microfinance and evidence-based interventions.
Austrian School Carl Menger (1840–1921), Friedrich Hayek (1899–1992), Ludwig von Mises (1881–1973) People value things differently; free markets find prices without government control. Individual choice, subjective value, and skepticism of central planning.
Behavioral Economics Amos Tversky (1937–1996), Daniel Kahneman (1934–2024) People make choices based on feelings and shortcuts, not pure logic (e.g., buying sale items you don’t need). Psychological biases shape decisions, challenging rational actor model.
New Institutional Economics Ronald Coase (1910–2013), Douglas North (1920–2015) Good rules and trust help economies work better (e.g., safe contracts encourage business). Institutions and transaction costs shape performance; norms and rules matter.
Public Choice Theory James Buchanan (1919–2013), Gordon Tullock (1922–2014) Politicians act in their own interest too, not just for the public (e.g., promising things to win votes). Politics analyzed with economic logic; actors are self-interested.
